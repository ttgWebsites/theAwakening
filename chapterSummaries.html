Chapter Summaries

  • Chapters 1 - 3
  • The Pontellier family is on vacation at a resort on Grand Isle.
  • The Pontelliers are a wealthy Creole family from New Orleans. The family consists of Leonce, a forty year old business man, Edna, his 28 year old wife, and their two sons Etienne and Raoul.
  • The resort is managed by Madame Lebrun and her two sons, Robert and Victor.
  • Leonce is reading the paper outside the doorway of the main house but returns to the house he has rented due to the noise of nearby birds, which belong to Madame Lebrun.
  • Edna approaches with Robert Lebrun from the beach. She gets her jewelry from her husband and he rebukes her for getting sunburned.
  • Leonce leaves to play pool. Edna and Robert stay and talk.
  • Edna leaves to get ready for dinner and Robert goes and plays with the Pontellier children.
  • Leonce returned at eleven that night, waking up Edna.
  • He told her the stories and gossip from the night, to which she responded sleepily, upsetting her husband.
  • Leonce checked on the boys and found that Raoul had a fever. He reproached Edna for her inattention.
  • Upset by her husbands rant, Edna went out to the porch and began to cry once he had fallen asleep.
  • She was not exactly sure why she was so upset but she felt "an indescribable oppressionâ€¦ filled her whole being."
  • She is eventually forced back inside by the mosquitoes.
  • Leonce sets off for a business trip the next morning. He gave his wife half the money from the previous night and left for the week.
  • He sent her back a box of fruit and candy, which she shared with the other ladies. She, reluctantly it seems, agreed with the women that Leonce is the "best husband in the world.
  • Chapters 4 - 6
  • Leonce Pontellier thinks about Edna's treatment of their children. He concludes she is not the "mother" type and observes the differences between his wife and the other women at Grand Isle.
  • He thinks about Adele Ratignolle, who "was the embodiment of every womanly grace and charm" and is the ideal mother.
  • Adele, Edna, and Robert sit and talk.
  • Edna feels uneasy when the conversation turns to Adele's pregnancy and childbirth. It is said that Edna does not feel at home in Creole society what with the way they openly discuss sensitive topics.
  • As they sit there Robert and Edna exchange "glances or smiles which indicated a certain advanced stage of intimacy."
  • It is said that Robert devotes himself to a woman each summer and no one is surprised that he picked Edna.
  • He speaks of the summer with Adele.
  • Edna is glad Robert does not "speak words of love" toward her because it would be "unacceptable and annoying."
  • Edna draws Adele and Robert puts his head on her arm. She pushes him off twice.
  • Both Adele and Edna dislike the drawing, because it does not look like Adele, and Edna discards it.
  • The Pontellier children approach with their nurse. They carry their mother's painting supplies into the house. They ask for some candy then leave again.
  • Adele puts away her sewing supplies. She complains she is feeling faint but soon recovers and heads to her house.
  • Robert convinces Edna to go swimming with him.
  • Edna realizes she wanted to go to the beach with Robert and does not know why she said no in the first place.
  • Edna begins to have her awakening. She "was beginning to realize her position in the universe as a human being, and to recognize her relations as an individual to the world within and about her."
  • It is said that the sound of the sea invites one to reflect.
  • Chapters 7 - 9
  • It is said that Edna "was not a woman given to confidences" meaning that she kept to herself, as far as her feelings went. Also, she knowingly lives a "dual life:" he appearance conforms to the standards of society while the "inward life questions."
  • Adele has influenced her to become a little more open.
  • They go to the Pontellier's bathhouse for some peace and quiet.
  • Edna stares out at the sea and Adele asks her what is on her mind.
  • Edna reminisces about a large meadow she walked through as a child, and how walking through the high grass reminded her of swimming through water.
  • Edna says "religion took a firm hold upon [her]" out of habit.
  • She expresses her thoughts that this summers has a sense of purposelessness.
  • Edna wasn't close with her sisters and her friends were private with their emotions like her.
  • Edna married Leonce hoping that the marriage would bring her to reality from "the realm of romance and dreams."
  • Edna's feelings toward her children are inconsistent. When they are away she feels "free of her responsibility."
  • She keeps most of these feelings to herself but confesses some to Adele.
  • Robert and the children return.
  • Robert walks Adele to her house and Edna stays with her children.
  • Adele warns Robert not to seriously pursue Edna, as she is a married woman and vulnerable, and unlike the Creole women she might take him seriously.
  • Robert looks for Edna on the beach but she isn't there. He returns home and talks with his mother, Madame Lebrun.
  • Madame Lebrun throws a party for the guests at the resort.
  • The Farival twins play a request for the crowd.
  • Adele then plays the piano and Edna sees a rush of emotions in picture form in her mind.
  • Robert then gets Mademoiselle Reisz to play for Edna. Edna is reduced to tears by her playing and is told she is the only one in the room with playing for.
  • It is suggested that they go swimming when nobody wants to leave the party.
  • Chapters 10 - 13
  • Edna notices a change in Robert's behavior. He spends entire days away from her.
  • Edna swims in the ocean independently for the first time. She then decides she wants to swim far out to "where no woman had swum before."
  • She is irritated that she had not discovered how easy swimming was earlier.
  • She believes that she has gone out too far and "a quick vision of death smote her soul." She then composes herself and swims back to shore.
  • Edna puts on her clothes and returns to the house, ignoring the cries of the other guests who wanted her to stay.
  • Robert catches up with Edna on her way back.
  • Edna says she might never experience another night like this one, with the flood of emotions and new experiences.
  • Robert stays with Edna at her house until Leonce returns.
  • "The first-felt throbbing of desire" are said to be felt in the silent moments spent waiting for Mr. Pontellier to return.
  • When Leonce returns Edna refuses to go inside to bed. She stubbornly denies his asking, pleading, and ordering.
  • She thinks back of the times she would have done as he asked without thinking about it, as it had become part of her daily life.
  • She does not know why she ever "yielded" to her husband's demands.
  • Leonce stay out with her all night, smoking cigars and drinking wine. He offers her wine twice, but she refuses.
  • Just before dawn she goes inside, unable to stay awake any longer.
  • Edna wakes up after a few hours of "troubled and feverish" sleep.
  • Most of the other guests are still asleep. She dresses and asks one of Madame Lebrun's to wake up Robert. The narrator mentions this is the first time Edna seeks out Robert, but neither think anything of it.
  • The take the boat to Cheniere to go to church.
  • While sailing Edna feels as if she has been freed from something that was tying her down "had snapped the night before," leaving her to go wherever she pleased.
  • Edna and Robert make plans for the next day.
  • While at church, Edna becomes drowsy and gets a headache. She and Robert go ousted.
  • He leads her to Madame Antoine's house so she can rest.
  • She sleeps and was up feeling as if she had slept for a long time.
  • Robert prepares a fowl for them to eat.
  • The sat under the orange tree outside the house until night listening to Madame Antoine's stories, then they sailed back to Grand Isle
  • Chapters 14 - 17
  • Edna returns from Cheniere and puts her son Entienne to sleep, who would not go to sleep for Adele.
  • Adele tells Edna that Leonce was worried when she did not return from Cheniere immediately and almost went to get her, but was assured by Monsieur Farival that she was resting and would be on her way soon. He then leaves to do business for a little bit.
  • Adele goes back to her house to be with her husband, who does not like to be left alone.
  • Robert and Edna put Entienne, who has fallen asleep, to bed.
  • Robert leaves and Edna waits for Leonce to return.
  • She thinks about how she has changed over this summer and regrets Robert leaving for the night.
  • When Edna arrives late to dinner one evening she discovers that Robert is leaving. He is going to Mexico that very night.
  • He didn't mention anything to Edna, causing her much surprise.
  • She asks him only when he is leaving then goes back to her house after eating some of her food.
  • The others stay and talk about the nature of Mexicans.
  • Edna busies herself with housework and declines Madame Lebrun's invitation for her to sit with them until Robert leaves.
  • Adele visits Edna and agrees that Robert showed Edna "very little consideration" by not telling her. She unsuccessfully tried to get Edna to go up to the Lebrun's house with her.
  • Robert comes to say goodbye to Edna and she tells him she is upset with the way he handled his departure.
  • Robert's replies are unenthusiastic and distant, even when she asks him if he will write to her. Which, she remarks, is unlike him.
  • As Robert leaves she tries not to cry, and relates their relationship to the infatuation she felt toward young men in her youth.
  • Edna cannot stop thinking of Robert and visits Madame Lebrun's house to look at his pictures. She reads a letter he sent to his mother and "experienced a pang of jealousy because he had written to his mother rather than to her."
  • Leonce saw Robert in town and Edna asks him many questions about their encounter.
  • Edna does not think anything of the questions she is asking her husband, because she does not care for Robert the way she cares for her Leonce.
  • She has always kept her feelings to herself, because they, after all, belong to her.
  • Edna thinks about a time when she and Adele discussed levels of devotion to their children.
  • Edna and Mademoiselle Reisz talk on the beach about how Edna is doing without Robert. The conversation moves to the Lebrun family, particularly about the other son, Victor, and their relationship.
  • Mademoiselle Reisz gives Edna her address in the city.
  • The Pontelliers have a nice house and Leonce, being materialistic, likes to walk around his house and look at his possessions, making sure everything is in its place.
  • On Tuesdays Edna had her "reception day" where she would entertain guests in her home.
  • One Tuesday Edna decided to go out, leaving no excuse, and did not see any of the visitors. Leonce is concerned that the guests, some wives of important businessmen, will be upset.
  • Angry and displeased with his meal, Leonce leaves to get dinner at the club.
  • Edna finishes her meal then goes to her room. In anger she throws down her wedding ring and stomps on it. She then throws a glass vase.
  • Chapters 18 - 21
  • Edna stays at home the next day instead of going into town to meet her husband to look at furniture.
  • She draws briefly then goes to visit Adele and shows her some of her drawings.
  • When Edna leaves she find that she does not want Adele's life to be her own. She feels "a pity for that colorless existence."
  • Edna "began to do as she liked" and one of those things was not staying home on Tuesdays for the "reception days."
  • Leonce is angry with his wife's disregard for the household. He brings up Adele and the way she handles her music and the household.
  • Leonce sometimes wonders if Edna is becoming "a little unbalanced mentally." He can see that she is acting like herself and not as the person society perceives.
  • Leonce leaves her alone as she asks, and goes to the office. Edna paints and sings the song that Robert sung on Grand Isle, thinking about the island.
  • It is said that Edna's mood goes from extreme joy to extreme sadness.
  • While experiencing a sad mood swing Edna goes to visit Mademoiselle Reisz to hear her play the piano.
  • Edna discovers that she has moved and goes to see Madame Lebrun to see if she has Mademoiselle Reisz's new address.
  • She talks to Victor Lebrun. He tells her of Robert's letters, which makes her sad to think that she did not receive one.
  • Edna gets the new address and leaves. Victor says "Some way she doesn't seem like the same woman."
  • Mademoiselle Reisz is happy to see Edna. She tells Edna of a letter she received from Robert about her. Edna asks to see it, but is denied.
  • Mademoiselle Reisz eventually gives in and gives Edna the letter, then she plays a song that Robert requested she play for Edna.
  • Edna cries in response to the music and asks if she may come again. Mademoiselle Reisz tells her to come whenever she feels like it.
  • Chapters 22 - 25
  • Mr. Pontellier stops by the house of his friend Dr. Mandelet to talk to him about Edna. He describes Edna's recent change in behavior and how she has been keeping to herself, but staying out of the house until dark.
  • They discuss Edna's family and their medical history until the doctor tells Leonce that is just a phase she is going through.
  • Dr. Mandelet tells Leonce to send Edna to her sister's wedding in two weeks but Leonce says she refuses to go, saying, "A wedding is one of the most lamentable spectacles on earth."
  • The doctor then tells Leonce just to leave her alone and that he will come to dinner at their house on Thursday to observe Edna's behavior.
  • After Leonce leaves it is said that Dr. Mandelet would have liked to ask Leonce if he suspects Edna of having an affair.
  • Edna's father comes to visit. It is said that the two aren't very close but have certain things in common.
  • He has come to get a wedding present for his daughter Janet and a suit for himself.
  • Edna entertains her father by sketching him, which he took very seriously, then taking him to one of Adele's musicals (Mademoiselle Reisz declined the invitation to play for him).
  • Leonce goes to the club instead. Adele says they should spend more time together; Edna replies saying they wouldn't have anything to talk about anyway.
  • Edna enjoyed having her father around as it kept her busy.
  • When Doctor Mandelet came to dinner he could not see anything relating to what Leonce had said to him a few days earlier.
  • They take turns telling stories. Dr. Mandelet, still suspecting Edna of maybe having an affair, tells of a patient who had an affair but returned to her husband.
  • Edna then tells of two lovers who paddle out to the Baratarian Islands and never return.
  • This does not ease the doctor's suspicions.
  • Edna and her father get into an argument because Edna refuses to go to her sister's wedding.
  • Edna's father then gets angry at Leonce for being too lenient with Edna, saying Leonce needs to "put his foot down."
  • Leonce decides to go to the wedding in Edna's absence.
  • Leonce is about to leave for business and Edna finally shows affection towards him again. She is likened to Adele in her looking after him and even cries when he leaves.
  • The children also leave for Leonce's mother's home, leaving Edna completely alone.
  • She explores inside and outside the house, eats alone, and takes to her reading again.
  • Edna's feelings toward being alone at her house change often: restful at times, feeling like life is passing her by at others.
  • Alcee Acrobin and Mrs. Highcamp invite Edna to the horse races, where she had seen when she went with her father.
  • Alcee takes to Edna and escorts her home, asking Edna to go to the races with him again. She regrets letting him leave and not asking him to stay.
  • After they go to the races again Alcee has dinner with Edna and is very bold about his attraction to her.
  • Edna sends him away after he kisses her hand, feeling that she has betrayed Robert, not her husband.
  • Chapters 26 - 29
  • Alcee writes Edna a letter to apologize. Edna is embarrassed that she reacted so severely to having her hand kissed so she dismisses the incident and invites him to come over whenever he would like.
  • Alcee then resumes his flirting and visits her almost every day.
  • Edna visits Mademoiselle Reisz and tells her she is going to rent a small house, which is dubbed "the pigeon house" because of it's size.
  • She says her house does not feel like home and she is "tired of looking after that big house."
  • Mademoiselle Reisz tells Edna that she does not believe her. Edna says she will like the "feeling of freedom and independence" in the new house.
  • Mademoiselle Reisz still does not completely believe Edna and it is said Edna is not entirely sure why she is moving either.
  • Edna is given Roberts latest letter and is surprised by the news that he is coming back.
  • After they discuss Edna's feelings and the notion of love Edna admits to Mademoiselle Reisz that she loves Robert.
  • Edna feels "glad and happy to be alive." She buys candy for her children and writes a letter that is "brilliant and brimming with cheerfulness" to Leonce, explaining her move to the smaller house.
  • Alcee visits Edna and she discusses her talk with Mademoiselle Reisz with him.
  • He kisses Edna and her feelings are described as "a flaming torch that kindled desire."
  • When Alcee leaves Edna feels "irresponsible." She looks at all the things her husband has provided for her.
  • She then thinks about Robert and her growing love for him.
  • With this kiss she feels like she understands life, but feels "neither shame nor remorse."
  • She feels regret that "it was not the kiss of love" that brought her to this realization.
  • Edna gets ready to move without hearing back from Leonce about what his opinion is on the matter. She only takes the things which she bought for herself.
  • Alcee visits while Edna is absorbed by her preparations. She has Ellen work in the same room to avoid being left alone with Alcee.
  • She informs him that the farewell dinner will be the day after tomorrow, and that she will see him again then "with eyes that at once gave him courage to wait and made it torture to wait."
  • Chapters 30 - 34
  • Edna only invites a few people to her dinner party. Among them are Mademoiselle Reisz, Victor Lebrun, Alcee, and Mr. Ratignolle (Adele couldn't come due to her advanced pregnancy).
  • It is also Edna's 29th birthday and they drink to her health.
  • It is said that Edna appears regal and independent.
  • Edna becomes upset when Victor, at the request of Mrs. Highcamp, a friend from the races, sings the song that Robert sang to her on Grand Isle, "Ah! Si tu savais!"
  • Edna puts her glass down so hard that it shatters and covers Victor's mouth with her hand when he doesn't stop.
  • He kisses the palm of her hand, which is covering his mouth, and agrees to stop singing.
  • The guests decide that it is time to leave.
  • Alcee Arobin remains with Edna and helps her close and lock up the house. After which they head over to the pigeon house.
  • Alcee has left flowers there to surprise her and they talk a little.
  • Alcee sits beside her and kisses her shoulder and "did not say goodnight until she had become supple to his gentle, seductive entreaties."
  • Leonce writes back to Edna, upset with the inadequate reasons she gave for moving. He is concerned that the move will cause suspicion of his "financial integrity."
  • To preserve his reputation he has an architect remodel his house, justifying his wife's move. He also notifies the paper that they may take a summer vacation while the construction is in process.
  • Edna visits her children at their grandmother's house. She cried out of happiness upon seeing them and has a hard time leaving them.
  • Adele comes and visits Edna, complaining that she feels neglected. She asks what happened at the dinner party after her husband left and comments on the small size of the house.
  • Adele is worried about how it looks for Edna to be living alone in the house, especially with Alcee visiting her, who has a bad reputation.
  • Later Edna seeks "refuge" with Mademoiselle Reisz. She isn't home so Edna, knowing the location of the hidden key, lets herself in.
  • After a while Robert Lebrun knocked on the door, much to Edna's surprise.
  • Edna is upset to learn that he has been in town for two days and asks why he never wrote to her. He says that he did not think his letters would interest her, but Edna doesn't believe him.
  • They leave and Robert walks Edna home. She invites him to stay for dinner. He refuses at first, but then agrees after seeing the pain in her face.
  • They discuss Robert's experiences in Mexico. He worked like a machine while thinking about Grand Isle. When asked what she has been doing Edna repeats his words.
  • The dinner feels ceremonious; they speak of Mexico and local events but not of each other.
  • After dinner Alcee comes by to see Edna while she is talking to Robert. Alcee speaks of the beauty of Mexican girls.
  • Robert leaves soon after, annoyed by Alcee.
  • Alcee invites Edna to go for a walk or drive but she sends him away.
  • She spends the rest of the night thinking of Robert and jealously pictures him with some Mexican girl.
  • Chapters 35 - 39
  • The next morning Edna tells herself that Robert loves her and will visit her that day.
  • She receives letters from her children, Leonce (saying that he will be back in march and they will go on a vacation then), and from Alcee ("to assure her of his devotion" which he trusts she returns).
  • She writes back to her children, evasively answers her husband's letter, and doesn't write back to Alcee.
  • Robert doesn't visit her that day or the next, though she is hopeful each morning and goes to bed miserable each night.
  • She did not actively look for him by going to Mademoiselle Reisz's or go to Madame Lebrun's.
  • One night she goes for a drive with Alcee. When they return home it is said that "he had detected the latent sensuality" which he unfolded through his "sense" of her "requirements."
  • That night she was not miserable, but she did not awaken with hope.
  • Edna and Robert happen to meet at cafe. Robert is surprised and "ill at ease" to see her.
  • Edna is straightforward about her disappointment that Robert has not visited her. She says he is selfish and does not consider her feelings.
  • He calls her cruel just for the sake of being cruel. Edna turns the talk to light conversation.
  • They go back to Edna's house and she kisses Robert and they profess their love for each other.
  • Robert tells Edna the reason he went to Mexico was because he loved her but couldn't have her. He tells her that he constantly thought of her and wants her to be his wife, and is upset with himself for thinking such a thing.
  • Edna tells him she is not one of Leonce's possessions and could "give [her]self wherever [she] choose."
  • A servant comes in and tells Edna Adele is in labor and wants her there. She tells Robert that she loves "no one but [him], and to stay and she will be back.
  • Adele is stressed and impatient with Dr. Mandelet's lateness.
  • Edna begins to remember the pain of her own childbirth, considering it torture. She now hates the "ways of Nature."
  • As Edna leaves Adele whispers to her to remember and think of her children.
  • Dr. Mandelet walks Edna home. He says Adele should have had an "unimpressionable" woman there instead of Edna.
  • When asked about the summer trip Edna responds by saying she isn't going and that she will not be forced into anything.
  • The doctor tells Edna that she seems to be in trouble and that she can talk to him in confidence. She declines, saying she does not feel the need to talk about it. She says she just wants her "own way," even if she has to upset others for it.
  • The doctor tells her to come talk to him and says goodnight.
  • Edna sat on the porch and thinks about the night's events and Adele's parting words. She decides to go in to Robert and to think of her kids tomorrow.
  • Robert had left, leaving a note saying "I love you. Good-by--because I love you."
  • She laid down on the sofa and stayed awake all night, never making a sound.
  • Edna goes to Grand Isle alone to rest.
  • She runs into Victor Lebrun and Mariequita, who are shocked to see her. They make plans to eat lunch and Edna tells them she wishes to go for a swim beforehand, even though they warn her the water is too cold.
  • She walks to the beach thinking of Robert and how she didn't want to be near anyone else. Then she thinks of her children and how she feels they have enslaved her soul.
  • She takes off her bathing suit and stands naked before the sea. She feels like a "new-born creature."
  • She swims out without worrying about going too far out. She grows exhausted.
  • She thinks of her family and how they think they own her, of Mademoiselle Reisz saying an artist must possess a "should that dares and defies," of Robert who would never understand her, and finally of Doctor Mandelet who "would have understood if she had seen him--but it was too late." Her strength completely fails and she thinks of her father and sister and drowns
